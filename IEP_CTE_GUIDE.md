## Understanding IEPs in CTE Settings

### What an IEP Is (In Plain English)

An **Individualized Education Program (IEP)** is a legal document that says:
- "This student has a disability"
- "Here's what the student needs to succeed"
- "Here's how we'll provide those supports"
- "Here's how we'll measure if it's working"

### Key Principle: Least Restrictive Environment (LRE)

**Source**: [Understood.org: What is LRE?](https://www.understood.org/en/articles/least-restrictive-environment-lre-what-you-need-to-know)

LRE means: **Students with disabilities should learn in the same classrooms as other students, with accommodations and supports as needed.**

**For CTE**: Your student with an IEP should be in your regular technical class, using the same equipment and learning the same skills as everyone else - just with accommodations to help them succeed.

### Accommodations vs. Modifications

**Important Distinction**:

| Accommodation | Modification |
|--------------|--------------|
| **Changes HOW student learns** | **Changes WHAT student learns** |
| Student learns same content | Student learns different/reduced content |
| Same grade/assessment | Alternative grade/assessment |
| Example: Extended time test | Example: Simplified project |
| Doesn't change what's learned | Changes learning objectives |

**For IEP**: Most students need accommodations. Some need modifications. The IEP specifies which.

## Common IEP Accommodations for CTE (Technical & Hands-On Learning)

### Time & Pacing Accommodations

**Extended Time**
- 25-50% extra time on tests, quizzes, projects
- **In CTE**: Give student extra time to complete hands-on tasks, troubleshooting, or written assessments
- **Example**: Normal 60-min lab ‚Üí 90 min for IEP student
- **Smarter approach**: Have station rotation so their extended work doesn't hold up group

**Reduced Workload**
- Student completes 70-80% of assignments
- Focuses on core competencies
- **In CTE**: Essential skills must be done fully; non-essential tasks can be reduced
- **Example**: All students do circuit assembly; IEP student skips documentation project

**Breaks/Movement**
- Allow frequent breaks (5-10 min per hour)
- Movement breaks during seated work
- **In CTE**: Natural in hands-on classes; student can step away to stretch
- **Smarter approach**: Schedule breaks at transition points naturally

### Instruction & Materials Accommodations

**Modified Materials**
- Simplified worksheets or checklists
- Larger font size
- Reduced reading level (grade 6 instead of grade 10)
- Visual aids, diagrams, or color-coded steps
- **In CTE**: Simplified job cards, numbered steps with pictures
- **Smarter approach**: One universal template with options for different reading levels

**Assistive Technology**
- Text-to-speech software reads instructions
- Speech-to-text for written responses
- Audio recordings of procedures
- Magnification software
- **In CTE**: Tech reads job card while student does hands-on work
- **Cost-effective**: Free apps like Natural Reader, TTS Reader

**Verbal/Oral Instruction**
- Preferential seating (closer to teacher)
- Directions repeated verbally
- One-on-one explanations
- Step-by-step guidance
- **In CTE**: Perfect fit - hands-on showing is already there
- **Smarter approach**: Use TA for one-on-one during group instruction

**Manipulatives & Concrete Materials**
- Use actual materials/tools for practice before testing
- Hands-on demonstrations
- Models or samples to touch/examine
- **In CTE**: This is your strength - you already do hands-on!
- **Example**: Let student practice circuit on breadboard before actual test

### Testing & Assessment Accommodations

**Modified Testing Conditions**
- Separate, quiet room
- One-on-one administration
- Untimed tests
- Reduced number of questions
- **In CTE**: Practical exams can be adapted
- **Example**: Instead of 20 troubleshooting scenarios, student does 10-12

**Alternative Assessment Formats**
- Practical demonstration instead of written test
- Oral exam instead of written
- Portfolio or project instead of test
- Checklist-based assessment
- **In CTE**: PERFECT for technical learning
- **Example**: Instead of "write how to..." ‚Üí "show me how to..." / "explain it to me"

**Use of Notes/Resources**
- Student can refer to notes during test
- Simplified reference sheet provided
- Formula sheet or procedure card
- Access to mentor/coach hints
- **In CTE**: Makes sense for technical trades
- **Example**: Electrician uses code book on the job; student can too

### Behavioral & Environmental Accommodations

**Preferential Seating**
- Closer to teacher for redirection
- Away from distractions
- Near peer model or TA
- **In CTE**: Place near teacher's station or near peer who's on-task

**Behavior Tracking**
- Positive behavior tracking system
- Frequent praise for following procedures
- Checklist of behavioral expectations
- **In CTE**: Combine with learning - "safety first" behavior chart

**Executive Function Support**
- Visual schedule posted
- Checklists for each task
- Reminders to start/check work
- Organization systems (labeled boxes, folders)
- **In CTE**: Task boards already useful; add visuals for IEP student

## Implementing Accommodations: Practical Examples in CTE

### Example 1: Electronics Lab - Student with Reading Disability

**IEP Accommodations**:
- Extended time (25%)
- Oral instructions
- Modified materials (simplified job card with pictures)
- Hands-on practice before assessment

**How You Execute**:
1. Before the lab, give student simplified 1-page job card with:
   - Pictures of each step (you draw or find online - reuse next year!)
   - 3-4 words max per step
   - Color-coded connections
2. Verbally review with student: "First you connect red to positive, yellow to negative"
3. Let them practice on breadboard while you work with class
4. TA checks their work, gives thumbs up/down
5. Assessment: Practical demo (student builds it) not written test
6. **Time saved**: One laminated card reused; TA handles, not you

### Example 2: Robotics - Student with Fine Motor Delays

**IEP Accommodations**:
- Extended time
- Assistive devices (modified tools, larger grips)
- Alternative assessment (verbal explanation vs. written)
- Breaks as needed

**How You Execute**:
1. Use adaptive equipment: larger screwdrivers, gripping tools (Etsy, $5-15)
2. Student does programming (strength) while peer builds (strength for peer)
3. No time pressure - they work at own pace
4. Assessment: "Explain what this code does" spoken aloud (you record, saves paper!)
5. Partner work naturally builds social/teamwork
6. **Time saved**: Peer-pairing reduces one-on-one demand

### Example 3: HVAC/Mechanical - Student with Anxiety/Processing Delays

**IEP Accommodations**:
- Extended time
- Reduced workload (focus on key systems)
- Breaks as needed
- Preferential location (near exit/quiet area)
- Simplified materials

**How You Execute**:
1. Give advance notice: "Monday we're diagnosing furnaces"
2. Show video/model the night before (preview = less anxiety)
3. Let student set breaks: "I need 5 min" is OK
4. Pair with patient peer
5. Focus on 3 furnace types (others do 5)
6. Assessment: Troubleshoot 2 scenarios (others do 4)
7. **Time saved**: Video once, reuse every year!

## At-Home Learning for CTE: Doing It Smarter

### The Challenge
Technical courses are hands-on. How can students learn at home?

### The Smart Solution: Tiered Options

**Tier 1: Concept/Theory (Easy at Home)**
- Video lectures on how systems work
- Reading/research assignments
- Online simulations or virtual labs
- Answering conceptual questions
- Practice problems
- **Platform**: YouTube, Khan Academy, or your own recordings

**Tier 2: Virtual Practice (Medium at Home)**
- Virtual labs and simulations
- Online training modules
- Digital troubleshooting scenarios
- **Examples**:
  - iCEV: [Career Tech Curriculum](https://www.icevonline.com/curriculum) - 3,400+ CTE lessons
  - ACTE: [Distance Learning Resources](https://www.acteonline.org/professional-development/opportunities/distance-learning-resources/)
  - Free simulations: Tinkercad, CircuitLab, PhET simulations

**Tier 3: Home-Based Hands-On (Creative at Home)**
- What can they do WITH WHAT THEY HAVE?
- **Sources**: [CTE Distance Learning: Hands-on Learning at Home](https://ctepolicywatch.acteonline.org/2020/04/cte-distance-learning-hands-on-learning-at-home.html)

**Examples by Course**:

| Course | At-Home Option |
|--------|----------------|
| **Electrical** | Build circuits with battery, LED, wires (costs $10 kit) |
| **Culinary** | Prepare a recipe, video technique, safety |
| **HVAC** | Watch/document home HVAC system, filters, settings |
| **Robotics** | Program using free software; use cardboard/LEGO |
| **Carpentry** | Basic woodworking: cutting, measuring, joinery with scrap |
| **Welding** | Video analysis of weld quality; practice safety |
| **Auto Tech** | Oil change, air filter, fluid checks on family car |

**IEP Consideration**: At-home work counts as practice, not mastery. In-class is still required for skill demonstration.

### Smart Approach for At-Home Work

**Pre-record everything once**:
- 5-min video: "How to diagnose a circuit"
- 3-min video: "Safety when using basic tools"
- Reuse every year; students watch on their time
- **Cost**: $0 (use phone camera + free editing)

**Use free platforms**:
- YouTube (unlisted videos for your class only)
- Google Classroom (posts materials, tracks submission)
- Canvas or Blackboard (if district uses)

**IEP Accommodation for At-Home Work**:
- Simplified checklist instead of full report
- 25% less problems to solve
- Video option: "Record yourself explaining" vs. "write explanation"
- Deadline: 5 days instead of 3

## The "Minimum" You Need to Do in Class

### Legal Minimum (From NJ Law)

You **MUST**:
1. Know what accommodations the student has
2. Implement them during instruction
3. Use them during assessment
4. Document that you did

You **MUST NOT**:
1. Ignore the IEP
2. Say "everyone does the same thing"
3. Refuse an accommodation
4. Grade unfairly because of disability

### Practical Minimum (Smart Approach)

**Here's the honest reality**: You can meet IEP requirements efficiently with smart systems.

**Minimal Daily Tasks**:
- 3 min: Check which students have IEPs (print list on clipboard)
- 2 min: Do you have the modified materials? (Yes = pre-prepared)
- 5 min: Give one-on-one instruction/check to IEP student(s)
- 2 min: Note if accommodation was used (just check a box)
- **Total: 12 minutes per day** (or less with smart systems)

**What You're NOT Required to Do**:
- ‚ùå Create totally different lessons (accommodate existing ones)
- ‚ùå Spend hours documenting (one checklist per month = enough)
- ‚ùå Teach separately (teach whole class; student in same class)
- ‚ùå Modify grading standards (same standard; different path to show it)
- ‚ùå Daily written notes (weekly note to file = sufficient)

**Where You Save Time**:
- TA handles data collection ("Did student use text-to-speech?" Y/N)
- Special Ed teacher co-plans (you don't plan alone)
- Reuse materials year after year (one job card = 5 years of use)
- Tech does the work (speech-to-text = no typing)
- Grouping naturally accommodates (hands-on work = built-in variety)

## Smart Systems That Save Time

### System 1: Pre-Made Accommodation Kit (30 min to create; years of use)

**For Your Course**, create one folder with:

**Folder: "Modified Materials"**
- Simplified job cards (large font, pictures, 3-4 steps)
- Checklists for common tasks
- Reference sheets with key info
- Assessment rubrics in simple language
- **Do once; use 5 years**

**Example - Electronics**:
```
CIRCUIT BUILD - Simplified Job Card (IEP)

1. Red wire ‚Üí positive terminal (PICTURE HERE)
2. Black wire ‚Üí negative terminal (PICTURE HERE)
3. LED ‚Üí positive & negative
4. Turn on power
5. Light = Success! ‚úì

DONE! Get teacher to verify.
```

**Cost**: 30 minutes; saves 5+ hours/year = NET TIME SAVED

### System 2: TA-Managed Accommodation Checklist

**Create simple tracking sheet**:
```
DATE: ____  STUDENT: ____

‚ñ° Extended time used? Y/N
‚ñ° Modified materials provided? Y/N
‚ñ° Oral instruction given? Y/N
‚ñ° Technology used (text-to-speech, etc.)? Y/N
‚ñ° Assessment modified? Y/N
‚ñ° Student attended whole lesson? Y/N

Notes: _______________
```

**Who fills it**: TA or peer buddy
**Time required**: 30 seconds per class
**Legal protection**: You're documenting compliance

### System 3: One Folder Per Student IEP

**Digital folder for each IEP student** with:
- Copy of their accommodations list (1 page)
- Their modified materials (job cards, etc.)
- Assessment variations for them
- Monthly documentation sheet (Y/N checkboxes)

**Location**: Drive or physical file
**Time to create**: 20 minutes per student
**Time to maintain**: 5 minutes per month
**Benefit**: Everything in one place; quick reference during chaos

### System 4: Buddy System

**Pair each IEP student with** a peer who:
- Models behavior/procedures
- Helps verbally explain concepts
- Provides social support
- Doesn't require teacher management

**Benefit**: Built-in support; develops leadership in peer; frees you up

## Monthly Documentation (Smarter Approach)

### What You MUST Document

NJ law requires you to show that:
1. Accommodations were provided
2. Student participated in instruction
3. Assessment used accommodations

### How to Do It Simply

**Monthly Check-in Sheet** (One page per student per month):

```
STUDENT: Maria G.
MONTH: February 2026

ACCOMMODATIONS PROVIDED:
‚ñ° Extended time (25%) - used in 8/10 labs
‚ñ° Simplified job cards - provided each day
‚ñ° Oral instructions - given before each activity
‚ñ° Breaks as needed - used 2-3 times/week

STUDENT PARTICIPATION:
‚úì Attended all lessons
‚úì Completed hands-on work
‚úì Participated in group activities
‚úì Took all assessments with accommodations

PROGRESS NOTES:
Maria is building confidence with circuit assembly. Extended time helps her verify her own work. Consider increasing complexity in March.

TEACHER SIGNATURE: _____ DATE: _____
```

**That's it**. One page = monthly documentation.
**Time**: 5 minutes to write
**Legal value**: High (shows you're implementing and tracking)

### What NOT to Document

‚ùå Daily notes on every interaction
‚ùå Detailed logs of behavior
‚ùå Lengthy descriptions
‚ùå Multiple copies

You're not required to create paperwork burden. A simple checklist = sufficient.

## Red Flags: When You Need Help

üö® **Reach out if**:
- You don't understand an accommodation
- The accommodation seems impossible in your setting
- The student isn't making progress despite accommodations
- You have concerns about the student's safety
- The student needs different accommodations than listed
- You need materials or technology

**Who to contact**:
1. **Special Education Teacher** (in your building) - First step
2. **Director of Special Education** (your district office)
3. **IEP Case Manager** (listed in the IEP document)
4. **Your principal** (who approves accommodations)

**Important**: It's OK to ask for help. Better to ask than to struggle.

## Summary: Legal Requirements + Practical Implementation

### What the Law Says

**Sources**:
- [N.J.A.C. 6A:14-3.7 - IEP Requirements](https://www.law.cornell.edu/regulations/new-jersey/N-J-A-C-6A-14-3-7)

- [Special Education Office](https://www.nj.gov/education/specialed/)
- [IDEA Federal Law](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/20/chapter-33)

**Your Requirement**:
You must provide accommodations and modifications listed in the IEP **immediately upon placement** - no delay. You're informed by your principal. You implement and document. That's it.

### What Good Implementation Looks Like

1. ‚úÖ Student with IEP is in your regular CTE class
2. ‚úÖ They have the same learning objectives as peers
3. ‚úÖ They get support/accommodations to help them reach those objectives
4. ‚úÖ You use the same assessments, maybe with accommodations
5. ‚úÖ They graduate with industry credentials like peers (modified path, same destination)

### What's Inefficient & Unnecessary

‚ùå Separate curriculum (don't do this)
‚ùå Hiding the student (include them)
‚ùå Daily documentation of every moment (not required)
‚ùå Denying an accommodation (illegal)
‚ùå Spending hours on IEP coordination (delegate/automate)
