## Understanding Behavior in CTE Settings

### Why CTE Is Different

Technical education classes have unique behavioral dynamics:

**Why behavior issues happen in CTE**:
1. **Hands-on work** - Less structured time, more movement
2. **Equipment/tools** - Safety concerns elevate stakes
3. **Frustration** - Students struggle with complex tasks
4. **Engagement** - Highly engaged OR completely disengaged (no middle ground)
5. **Relevance** - Students want to see real-world application
6. **Peer work** - Group projects = social dynamics

**This is NORMAL**. You're not bad at classroom management if behavioral issues arise in CTE. It's the nature of the setting.

## Common Behavioral Issues in CTE & Smart Responses

### Issue 1: Disrespectful/Inappropriate Language

**What it looks like**:
- Swearing during frustration
- "This is stupid" about assignment
- Talking back to you
- Sarcastic/condescending tone

**Why it happens**:
- Student is frustrated with task
- Showing frustration to peers (not necessarily at you)
- Doesn't realize tone is disrespectful
- Trying to save face in front of peers

**Smart Response** (Prevention First):
1. **Prevent it**: Don't assign frustrating tasks without success path
   - Break down complex procedures step-by-step
   - Show success early
   - Celebrate small wins

2. **In the moment**: Don't escalate
   - Stay calm (don't match their energy)
   - Use positive framing: "I can tell this is frustrating. Let's figure it out together."
   - Move closer (proximity helps calm)
   - Private conversation (not public calling out)

3. **After**: Address once calm
   - "I noticed you said [what they said] when the circuit didn't work. I know it's frustrating, but we use different language here. What can you try differently next time?"
   - This is TEACHING, not punishing
   - Don't hold grudge

**Documentation**:
```
Date: 2/10/2026
Student: Marcus
Incident: Used profanity ("This is f***ing stupid") when circuit test failed
Response: Moved student to side; quiet conversation; offered alternative troubleshooting approach
Outcome: Student apologized; reengaged with task
```

### Issue 2: Defiance/Refusing to Follow Directions

**What it looks like**:
- "I'm not doing that"
- Ignoring your instruction
- Arguing with you about procedures
- Refusing to leave area when asked

**Why it happens**:
- Student feels disrespected (real or perceived)
- Doesn't understand why they need to do it
- Previous negative interaction with you
- Peer pressure (can't back down in front of friends)
- Power struggle over small thing

**Smart Response** (According to Responsive Classroom & PBIS Research**):

**IMPORTANT**: Defiance is often a call for help, not an attack on you. Don't take it personally.

**In the moment**:
1. **De-escalate** (don't escalate)
   - Lower your voice (don't match their volume)
   - Use neutral body language (don't point fingers, stand over them)
   - Give them an out: "I can see you're upset. Let's take a step back."

2. **Preserve dignity**
   - Don't argue in front of peers
   - Move conversation private
   - Don't demand immediate compliance
   - Offer choices when possible

3. **Get the behavior, not the agreement**
   - "I need you to step away from the equipment right now"
   - Don't wait for apology or agreement
   - Once they comply, debrief later

4. **Reconnect after**
   - One-on-one conversation (lunch, after class)
   - "I know we had conflict today. I want us to figure this out."
   - Listen to THEIR perspective
   - Many defiant students are acting out because they feel they're not heard

**Documentation**:
```
Date: 2/10/2026
Student: Jamal
Incident: Refused to put on safety glasses; told me "That's stupid. I'm not wearing them."
Response: Moved away from equipment. Quietly said "Safety first - non-negotiable." Gave him 30 seconds.
He put them on. After class, brief conversation: "What was going on?" He said he forgot them and was
embarrassed asking. Solution: safety equipment reminder board so he can grab without asking.
Outcome: Positive reconnection. Wore glasses without issue rest of week.
```

### Issue 3: Not Paying Attention/Off-Task Behavior

**What it looks like**:
- Talking to friends instead of working
- On phone/social media
- Doing other class work
- Staring into space
- Not showing up (chronically absent/tardy)

**Why it happens**:
- Material is too easy (bored)
- Material is too hard (frustrated, giving up)
- Peer distraction (sitting with friends)
- Lack of connection to relevance
- External issues (home, relationships, anxiety)
- ADHD or other processing differences

**Smart Response**:

**Prevention**:
1. **Engagement**: Hook them from day 1
   - Start with "why this matters" (real career application)
   - Show how it connects to actual jobs they might want
   - Let them see themselves in the content

2. **Movement**: Technical ed has this built-in, but ensure work isn't monotonous
   - Rotate between stations
   - Mix instruction types
   - Include hands-on with conceptual

3. **Relationship**: Know your students
   - What are they interested in?
   - Where do they want to work?
   - What's happening in their life?
   - Brief check-ins go a long way

4. **Seating**: Strategic placement
   - Don't seat friends together on first day (see who clicks)
   - Separate known distractions
   - Seat disengaged students near you or strong peer

5. **Clear expectations**: Boredom often = no structure
   - What should they be doing every minute?
   - Make it visible (posted schedule, task board)

**In the moment**:
- Proximity: Stand near off-task student
- Eye contact: Get their attention
- Redirect: Clear, specific instruction ("Maria, I need you focused on the circuit build")
- Praise when back on task: "Thank you for staying focused"
- Private conversation if pattern: Find out why (bored? confused? personal issue?)

**Documentation**:
```
Date: 2/10/2026
Student: DeShawn
Incident: Off-task during independent work time - talking with peers, not completing assignment
Pattern: Happens when task is repetitive or he doesn't see application
Response: Checked in: "What's the hold-up?" He said he didn't see why we're doing it.
Explained connection to solar panel technician job he's interested in.
Outcome: Reengaged immediately when saw relevance. Follow-up: provide context for all procedural work.
```

### Issue 4: Lack of Effort/Motivation

**What it looks like**:
- Half-finished work
- "I don't care about this class"
- Gives up easily when something is hard
- Assumes they'll fail
- Doesn't turn in work

**Why it happens**:
- Negative self-image ("I'm not good at this")
- Previous failures (gave up before, keeps giving up)
- Doesn't see point (no career relevance)
- Overwhelmed by workload
- Undiagnosed learning differences
- External pressures (work, home, relationships)

**Smart Response**:

**Prevention**:
1. **Show early success**
   - Start with easier tasks to build confidence
   - Celebrate small wins publicly
   - Show growth (not just grades)

2. **Make it relevant**
   - Connect to their goals
   - Bring in guest speakers from careers
   - Show what they'll make/earn

3. **Manage workload**
   - Is it realistic amount?
   - Are steps clear?
   - Do they have resources?

4. **Build relationship**
   - Let them know you believe in them
   - "I know you can do this"
   - "I'm here to help"

**In the moment**:
- Don't accept "I don't care" - dig deeper
- Pair with peer/TA for support
- Break task into smaller chunks
- Offer choice: "Do you want to start here or here?"
- Celebrate effort, not just results

**Documentation**:
```
Date: 2/10/2026
Student: Asia
Pattern: Low effort, minimal engagement, saying she'll never pass
Response: Had conversation about her goals (wants to be electrician). Explained how this
class connects to that. Offered to let her work with peer who's strong at circuits.
Setup: Weekly check-ins to track progress, celebrate small wins.
Outcome: Effort increasing. Now asking clarifying questions instead of shutting down.
```

## The PBIS Framework: Prevention Over Punishment

### What Is PBIS?

**Source**: [Center on PBIS - What is PBIS?](https://www.pbis.org/pbis/what-is-pbis)

PBIS = **Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports**

It's not soft. It's smart. It uses data and research to prevent behavior problems before they start.

**Three Tiers**:

**Tier 1: Universal (All Students)**
- Clear expectations posted
- Positive behavior reinforced
- Routines and procedures taught explicitly
- Consequence for not following is swift, consistent, and logical
- **Result**: 80-90% of students never need extra help

**Tier 2: Targeted (10-15% of Students)**
- Students identified as struggling with behavior
- Extra support, mentoring, check-in systems
- **Example**: Marcus gets a daily check-in with you, "How'd your morning go?"
- **Result**: Most students respond to extra attention

**Tier 3: Intensive (3-5% of Students)**
- Students with most serious/frequent behavior issues
- Specialized plans, counselor involvement, parent partnership
- **Your role**: Provide data, follow the plan
- **Result**: These students get real help, not just punishment

### Your Classroom PBIS Setup

**Step 1: Three Classroom Expectations**

Pick 3 (not 10). CTE-specific:

**Example**:
1. **SAFE** - Respect equipment, yourself, others
   - Wear safety equipment
   - Follow procedures
   - Ask before touching equipment
   - Respect peers' space

2. **ENGAGED** - Full participation
   - Be present and focused
   - Ask questions
   - Help peers
   - Try your best

3. **PROFESSIONAL** - Act like someone at a job
   - Respectful language
   - Follow instructions
   - Handle mistakes calmly
   - Own your work

**Step 2: Teach These**

Don't assume students know. SHOW them:
- "Being SAFE means... [demonstration]"
- Explain why (health, jobs, careers)
- Give examples and non-examples
- Practice it

**Step 3: Catch Them Being Good**

This is key. Most teachers only notice bad behavior.

- Praise specific behavior: "I noticed you asked for help instead of giving up - that's ENGAGED"
- Public recognition: Write name on board, call out, small reward
- Frequency: Catch more good than bad (at least 3:1 ratio)

**Step 4: Consistent Consequences**

**For minor issues** (not following expectations):
1. **Redirect**: "We use respectful language here. Can you say that differently?"
2. **Reminder**: "Remember, SAFE means following procedures. Let's do that."
3. **Reflection**: "What expectation did you not meet? What will you do next time?"

**For repeated issues**:
- Communicate with parent
- Create behavior plan (with student input)
- Daily check-in system
- Maybe special seating/mentor

**For serious issues** (safety risk, defiance):
- Remove from situation (safety first)
- Separate conversation later
- Involve administration
- Document everything

## Smarter Not Harder: Behavior Management Systems

### System 1: Daily Expectation Reminder (30 seconds/day)

**Do this at start of class**:
- Visual: Poster with 3 expectations
- Verbal: "Today we're focusing on SAFE. That means..."
- Practice: Quick example
- **Result**: 50% fewer behavior issues

### System 2: Positive Reinforcement System (2 min/week)

**Pick one**:
- **Point system**: 3-5 points per day for expectations, trade for rewards
- **Praise tracker**: Give specific praise every class (at least 5 students)
- **Shoutout board**: Name on board = lunch privilege or free pass
- **Simple**: One system, not complicated

**Key**: Catch good behavior BEFORE it becomes bad

### System 3: Tier 2 Check-In (5 min/week per student)

**For students showing struggles**:
- 2-minute conversation
- "How's it going? Anything I can help with?"
- Positive note about progress
- One goal for next week
- **Result**: Student feels supported, behavior improves

### System 4: Documentation (5 min/month per student)

- One page per month
- Checkboxes and bullets, not essays
- What went well, what's being worked on
- **Purpose**: Legal protection + parent communication ready
