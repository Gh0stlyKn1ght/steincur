## ğŸ’¼ Types of Partnerships & How to Use Them

### **Type 1: Guest Speaker** (Easiest to Start)

**What**: Professional from industry visits class, talks about their job

**Time Commitment**: 1-2 hours (including travel)

**How to Set Up**:
```
1. Email company/contact: "Would you have 1-2 hours available to visit our class?"
2. Confirm date/time/topic
3. Prepare students (background, questions to ask)
4. Host guest (introduce, facilitate Q&A)
5. Follow-up (students write thank-you notes)
```

**Student Benefit**: See real careers, ask questions, understand job requirements

**Your Benefit**: Credibility boost ("This person has a real job doing what you're learning"), motivation boost for students

**Timing**: 1-2 per marking period per course (6-12 total per year)

**Example**:
- Week 3 of Electronics: Electronics repair technician talks about troubleshooting
- Week 7 of Robotics: Automation engineer talks about how robots are designed

---

### **Type 2: Equipment Donation/Partnership**

**What**: Company donates, discounts, or loans equipment for your lab

**Examples**:
- Discounted robotics kits
- Donated tools or test equipment
- Loaned 3D printer or specialized equipment
- Free software licenses (educational discount)

**How to Set Up**:
```
1. Identify what you need (be specific)
2. Research companies that make/sell it
3. Email: "We're starting a CTE program. Could you donate [specific item]
   or provide educational discount?"
4. Explain student benefit and program goals
5. Provide tax documentation if they want write-off
6. Recognize donation publicly (thank-you letter, school newsletter, class mention)
```

**Student Benefit**: Better equipment = better learning, updated technology

**Your Benefit**: Reduced costs, better program quality, company recognition

**Timing**: Ongoing throughout year as needed

**Examples**:
- Tinkercad Pro account (free for educators)
- Arduino starter kit kits at bulk discount
- 3D printer on loan from local maker space
- Multimeters donated by electronics supply store

---

### **Type 3: Mentorship/Job Shadowing**

**What**: Student(s) meet with professional 1-on-1 or job shadow

**Duration**:
- Mentorship: 1-2 hours/month for semester/year
- Job shadowing: 4-8 hours (day or two)

**How to Set Up**:
```
1. Identify interested student (advanced, serious about career)
2. Contact company: "I have a student interested in [career].
   Could they spend [time] learning from your [professional]?"
3. Set expectations (student will prepare, follow company rules, etc.)
4. Arrange logistics (location, parking, timing)
5. Student prepares (questions, professional behavior, follow-up)
6. Debrief with student afterward
```

**Student Benefit**: Real-world learning, professional network, resume material, possible internship

**Your Benefit**: Advanced students have realistic career pathway, program credibility

**Timing**: For advanced students/seniors, ongoing throughout year

**Examples**:
- Robotics student shadows programmer at local tech company (monthly)
- Electronics student job-shadows repair technician (one day)
- Engineering student mentored by design engineer (1 hr/month)

---

### **Type 4: Internship/Work-Study**

**What**: Student works at company part-time (20-30 hrs/week) for credit

**Duration**: Semester or full year

**How to Set Up**:
```
1. Work with guidance counselor on logistics (school credit/pay)
2. Identify students interested in specific field
3. Contact companies: "I have students ready for internships. Would you consider
   hosting one for [duration] doing [type of work]?"
4. Establish learning objectives with company supervisor
5. Check in monthly with student and company
6. Evaluate student performance and reflection
```

**Student Benefit**: Paid experience, real job skills, resume, possible job offer

**Your Benefit**: Students graduate career-ready, company recognizes your program

**Timing**: Usually seniors, spring semester or full year

**Examples**:
- Electronics student interns at repair shop (spring semester)
- Engineering student interns at manufacturing company (full year)
- Career student interns at various companies (multiple placements possible)

---

### **Type 5: Curriculum Advisory Board** (Most Valuable)

**What**: Industry professionals review your curriculum, advise on content/skills

**Frequency**: 2-4 meetings per year

**How to Set Up**:
```
1. Email 3-5 professionals: "Would you serve on an advisory board for my CTE program?
   We'd meet 2-3 times per year to ensure curriculum reflects real job requirements."
2. Send agenda before each meeting
3. Ask for input on: skills students need, equipment worth investing in, industry trends
4. Thank them publicly and implement suggestions
```

**Student Benefit**: Curriculum stays relevant to actual jobs

**Your Benefit**: Validation that you're teaching right skills, industry support, feedback

**Timing**: Schedule meetings strategically (fall, mid-year, spring)

**Examples**:
- 4 professionals (engineer, technician, manager, entrepreneur) meet quarterly
- Advisory board gives input on what skills to prioritize
- Recommendations guide curriculum updates and resource allocation
